When AI Disproved an 80-Year-Old Erdős Conjecture — Why This Matters Beyond Mathematics

2026-05-24 · ~4 min · AI Research

If you have been following AI news, you are probably used to hearing about AI writing poetry, generating code, or creating art. But something far more surprising happened recently: an AI model successfully disproved a mathematical conjecture that had stood unchallenged for 80 years. At the same time, Google DeepMind unveiled an AI Co-Mathematician that set a new high score on the FrontierMath benchmark. These two developments, arriving within days of each other, tell us something profound about where AI reasoning is heading — and it goes way beyond math.

The Conjecture That Stood for 80 Years

Paul Erdős was one of the most prolific mathematicians of the 20th century. Born in Hungary, he published over 1,500 papers and collaborated with nearly 500 researchers throughout his lifetime. He was famous for posing hundreds of conjectures — statements he believed to be true but could not prove. Many remain open to this day. One of them, the Unit Distance Conjecture from 1946, asked a deceptively simple question: if you place n points on a plane, how many pairs of points can be exactly the same distance apart? Erdős believed that the number of such "unit distance" pairs grows much more slowly than the number of points. In plain language, he thought this kind of structure should not be easy to create. For 80 years, nobody could prove him right or wrong. Then an OpenAI reasoning model entered the picture.

How an AI Did What Mathematicians Could Not

Here is what makes this remarkable: OpenAI did not build a specialized mathematical system for this task. The model that accomplished this is a general-purpose reasoning model — the same kind that answers everyday questions. What it discovered was an infinite family of point arrangements in higher-dimensional lattices with special symmetry properties, which could then be mapped down into two dimensions. The result was a structure that produced more unit-distance pairs than Erdős had predicted. Think of it like discovering a way to fold paper in higher dimensions and then pressing it flat onto a table, revealing patterns with symmetries that human mathematicians had never conceived. OpenAI did not announce this immediately. They contacted several leading mathematicians to verify the proof before going public. Noga Alon, a renowned mathematician, wrote a companion paper confirming the result on arXiv. Another mathematician, Will Sawin, has already built upon this work to extend the findings further. This is not just an AI getting lucky. It is an AI system demonstrating genuine mathematical creativity.

DeepMind's AI Co-Mathematician: A Research Partner, Not a Calculator

Around the same time, Google DeepMind published research on their AI Co-Mathematician — a multi-agent system designed specifically to be a research partner for working mathematicians. Built on Gemini as its backbone, the system operates through an interactive workbench where mathematicians can have real-time back-and-forth conversations with the AI. The results are impressive: it scored 48% on FrontierMath Tier 4 (23 out of 48 problems), a new high score among all AI systems. But what matters more than the score is how it is being used in practice. A mathematician from Oxford used the system to solve a 60-year-old problem from the Kourovka Notebook — a famous collection of open mathematical problems that had resisted attempts for decades. A key design choice is that the benchmark rewards exploration and penalizes lucky guesses, making it particularly suited for work that requires genuine multi-step reasoning rather than pattern matching.

Why This Matters for Everyone

You might think discrete geometry and FrontierMath are abstract topics with no real-world relevance. But the implications reach further than you might expect. First, it shows that AI is beginning to understand abstraction at a level approaching professional mathematicians. This is not about pattern matching anymore — it is about creating novel structures that humans have never seen. Second, this capability has direct implications for cryptography and smart contract verification. Both of these fields are built on the foundations of discrete mathematics. As AI gets better at this kind of reasoning, both the creation and the breaking of encryption systems will evolve. Third, it signals a shift in AI reasoning — from answering closed questions to generating new knowledge. Think of it as the difference between a student who excels at exams and a researcher who can formulate entirely new questions.

The Bigger Picture

Both OpenAI and DeepMind are pointing in the same direction: AI as a research partner. Not a faster calculator, but a collaborator that thinks from different angles and proposes approaches that humans might never consider. To be clear, there are still significant limitations. The latest models still score below 1% on ARC-AGI-3, and systematic reasoning errors remain a problem. But what happened with the Erdős conjecture proves that under the right conditions, AI can genuinely create something unprecedented. For developers and researchers paying attention, the pattern worth noting is iterative exploration: have the AI generate a hypothesis, test it, refine it, and repeat. This is not just a technique for mathematics — it is a framework that applies to any complex problem domain. Eighty years the conjecture stood. Then AI came along and resolved it within a single research cycle. That is not just a math story. It is a story about how the process of discovery itself is changing — and it is changing faster than most people realize.
